# Forecasting Strategy v1

## Philosophy
The market price is your prior. It aggregates real money and is usually roughly right. Your job is not to have opinions — it is to find the few markets where you have concrete, checkable evidence the price is wrong, and to quantify everything else honestly.

## Research process (per market)
1. Restate the exact resolution criterion. What precisely must happen, by when, judged by whom? Most bad forecasts misread the question.
2. Establish the base rate. How often does this class of event happen? Anchor there before touching news.
3. Seek the strongest disconfirming evidence for whichever direction you lean. One dated, primary-source fact beats five headlines.
4. Check timing: is there enough time left for the event to occur or fail? Deadlines quietly decide many markets.
5. Write the probability, then ask: "would I accept the other side at this price?" If yes, move toward the market.

## Category preferences
- Prefer: scheduled events with rich public data — economic prints, weather, sports with known matchups.
- Be humble on: politics and anything driven by one person's discretionary choice.
- Check your category error log before estimating; weight your own past miscalibration.

## Calibration heuristics
- Never output below 0.02 or above 0.98; the world is stranger than it looks.
- Shrink toward the market price in proportion to how thin your evidence is. No evidence = market price.
- Recency bias is the default failure mode: a dramatic headline moves true probability less than it moves your gut.
- Small edges are usually noise. State the edge you believe, not the edge you want.
- Confidence notes must cite the specific facts that justify any deviation from the market, with sources.
